DESIGN FEEDBACK

Most design feedback is just noise.

4 before / after shifts that fix it.

01 / 07
figr.design @figr.design
The Problem
✗ What Happens

Everyone gives opinions.

Design reviews fill up with preferences. "I'd make it bigger." "I don't like that color." No one asks what problem the design is solving.

✓ What Should Happen

Feedback anchors to goals.

Every critique connects to a user need or a business goal. If you can't explain why the change matters to the user, the feedback doesn't count.

02 / 07
figr.design @figr.design
Shift 01
✗ Before

You present work and wait.

The designer shows up, shares the screen, and the room fires off reactions. Whoever speaks first sets the tone for the whole review.

✓ After

You frame the problem first.

Before showing anything, the designer states: what problem this solves, who it's for, and what one question the team should answer. Reviews become focused.

03 / 07
figr.design @figr.design
Shift 02
✗ Before

Feedback is about the design.

The team reacts to what they see — layout, color, copy. The conversation circles visuals without ever reaching the underlying logic.

✓ After

Feedback is about the decision.

Good critique asks: "What decision does this design make, and is it the right one?" Visual details follow from good decisions — not the other way around.

04 / 07
figr.design @figr.design
Shift 03
✗ Before

Everyone's a stakeholder.

Whoever is in the room gets a vote. Feedback from a PM, a dev, and a CMO all carry equal weight. The strongest opinion wins.

✓ After

Roles are clear before the session.

Before the review: who decides, who advises, who observes. A RACI takes 5 minutes to align. It turns a discussion into a decision.

05 / 07
figr.design @figr.design
Shift 04
✗ Before

The designer takes notes.

The designer is simultaneously defending their work, processing critique, and writing down changes. They leave the room with a list they didn't choose.

✓ After

The designer makes a call.

At the end of the review, the designer states: what they'll change, what they won't, and why. Publicly. In the room. Critique becomes a tool, not a verdict.
